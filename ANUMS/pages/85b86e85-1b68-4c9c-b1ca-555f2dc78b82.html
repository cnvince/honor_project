New Caledonia: negotiating postcolonial identities in the shadow of the EU

Date & time

14 May 2012
4:00pm - 5:30pm

New Caledonia: negotiating postcolonial identities in the shadow of the EU

Event category: 
Monday SLS seminar
Location: 
Baldessin Precinct Building, W3.03
Presenter: 
Dr Peter Brown, ANU

Europe’s long colonial history has led to many kinds of special relations to former colonies and dependencies of the EU member states. One particular type is the so-called 'OCT arrangement'; i.e. the unique legal status of the EU’s twenty-five ‘Overseas Countries and Territories’. These OCTs, all islands scattered throughout the world’s oceans, are constitutionally tied to a member state (Denmark, France, the Netherlands, the UK) without being formally part of the EU.

The European Commission recently proposed a comprehensive reform of the special OCT arrangement for the period 2013-2020. On the one hand, this reform project could be seen as an attempt to close the chapter on Europe’s ‘colonial legacy’, particularly given that a number of the OCTs are now more affluent (per capita) than the average EU member state. On the other hand, it could also be seen as a way for Europe to continue to exert influence in distant parts of the world, through the imposition of ‘European values’.

Yet these territories are also engaged in constructing their own identities – separate and distinct from their former colonisers. In 2009, a majority of the people of Greenland voted in favour of increased independence from Denmark; similarly, in the Pacific, the French islands of New Caledonia and French Polynesia have strong independence/autonomy movements. Conversely, in the Caribbean, Aruba and the Netherlands Antilles have successfully resisted Dutch moves for them to become formally sovereign, while some United Kingdom Overseas Territories in the Caribbean, engaged in various kinds of offshore financial activities, have other reasons to wish to remain free from outside regulation.

This paper will consider the case of New Caledonia, where a quasi civil war lasted several years in the 1980s over the question of independence (and social and racial divisions). The Matignon Accords (1988) restored peace; the Noumea Accord (1998) introduced political and constitutional change, and the island is due to organise a referendum on ‘independence’ in the period 2014-2018. Yet during the week it chaired the OCT meeting with the EU held in Noumea in March last year, to map out the future of the EU-OCT arrangement, the New Caledonian government fell amidst the worst political crisis the island had known since the civil war years of the 1980s. How then to view this far-flung member of the ‘European family’?

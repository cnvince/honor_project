Research interests

The South Pacific (particularly Timor-Leste, Bougainville and Tonga); war, conflict and post-conflict reconstruction and reconciliation; state, nation and peace-building; constitution-making; comparative constitutional law; ethnicity and nationalism; the link between ‘liberal’ and ‘local’ approaches to governance, justice, development and security; and the relationship between globalization, trade and development.

Biography

Dr Joanne Wallis completed her PhD in the Department of Politics and International Studies at the University of Cambridge.  Her PhD research considered the role that constitution-making plays in building state and nation in post-conflict societies, using case studies of Timor-Leste and Bougainville (an autonomous region of Papua New Guinea).  Her research has a particular emphasis on the opportunities for engagement between 'liberal' and 'local' approaches to law, governance, justice, development and security. 

Joanne has taught at the Australian Command and Staff College, University of Cambridge, University of Melbourne, Swinburne University, and Ormond College and Trinity College, University of Melbourne.  She is editing (with Andrew Carr) a book for Georgetown University Press, Asia-Pacific Security after the 'Pivot', inspired by her role as Convener of the Asia-Pacific Security program in the Strategic and Defence Studies Centre.

In October and November 2012 Joanne was a Visiting Scholar at the East-West Center, Honolulu.  From January 2009 to January 2012 she was an Honorary Fellow of the School of Social and Political Sciences at the University of Melbourne.  From November 2008 to February 2009 she was a Visiting Scholar at the State, Society and Governance in Melanesia Project at the ANU. In 2006 she was a Fulbright Scholar at the Walker Institute of International and Area Studies at the University of South Carolina. She has been an Editor of the Melbourne Journal of Politics and Secretary of the Melbourne Journal of International Law. 

Joanne has also conducted research consultancies for Australian and international NGOs, and writes analyses for a leading global political risk advisory service. 

From 2002-2004 Joanne worked as a lawyer at Allens Arthur Robinson (now Allens Linklaters) in Melbourne, and she is admitted as a barrister and solicitor in the Supreme Court of Victoria and High Court of Australia. During that time she also undertook pro bono legal work on behalf of persons seeking asylum in Australia, and was a member of the Victoria Committee of Australian Lawyers for Human Rights.

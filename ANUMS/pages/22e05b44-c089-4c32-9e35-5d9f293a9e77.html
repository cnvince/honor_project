Biography

Sharon Peoples was appointed convener on Museum Education and Heritage Interpretation in 2011 and as lecturer in the Internship Program of the Liberal Arts at the Research School of Humanities in 2008. She completed her PhD in 2008 in Fashion Theory within the Art History Department at the ANU. Her thesis investigated the issue of appearance and power in military uniforms in the eighteenth century. While this project began by examining cross-dressing in the military, the research turned to the formation of the 'normative' masculine body and examined how a female body might inhabit, not only such an institutional masculine dress, but also what Marcel Mauss termed 'techniques of the body'. Sharon maintains a profile as an exhibiting artist, lecturing in textiles at the ANU School of Art, curating textile exhibitions and writing in the crafts. Sharon worked with international architect, Romaldo Giugola, and completed the religious vestments for St Patrick's Cathedral, Parramatta. She has designed and completed two major commissions working with the ACT Embroiderers Guild: The Crimson Thread, National Museum of Australia, Canberra, and Patterns of Migration, National Maritime Museum of Australia, Sydney. She also lectured and tutored in the Art History Department ANU in 2005, was a craft critic for the Canberra Times from 2006-09. She is currently co-authoring The Fashion Studies Book (Routledge) with Professor Jennifer Craik.

Researcher's projects

The Fashion Studies Book' (Routledge 2012) co-authored with Prof Jennifer Craik which investigates fashion industry, education, and histories; identity and self; politics of fashion; and social change.

Past student projects

Host Institutions

From the list below it must be understood that some students bring with them a wealth of experience and qualifications that allows them to work on projects at a professional level. As well the size of institutions influenced the specificity of projects. At smaller institutions students tend to get a broader overview and are required to multi-task; whereas those in the larger institutions are directed to very specific projects.

Some of the institutions and the projects that students have participated in the Liberal Arts internship program (which derived from the museums and collections internship program) in the last two years include:

National Museum of Australia Internships with our industry partner include projects assisting with research and administration for future exhibition Warakurna: All the Stories got into our Minds and Eyes, including developing graphic and text hierarchy and Warakurna history paintings. 

National Gallery of Australia In the last four years we have had four students at the National Gallery. The most recent was a Maori student working on the Pacific Collection; another wrote an acquisition proposal for a Charles Condor painting as well as a catalogue entry for the Turner to Monet: The Triumph of Landscape exhibition. The second and third students worked in the exhibition design department of the gallery.

National Library of Australia Students have worked in education programs, producing learning kits on Indonesian popular culture magazines, assessing the acquisitions proposals in the Pictures Collection of the library. As well one student worked on the potential for social media as a means of publicity for the 2008 Vivid exhibition.

Australian War Memorial Installation of exhibitions within the Vietnam Gallery was the project for this student at the memorial. This included working with the registration process from objects moved from storage to exhibition case. Another student worked on the photographic collections

Other cultural institutions in the past include: National Archives of Australia, Old Parliament House, and Canberra Museum and Art Gallery:

Interstate:

Melbourne Museum This institution gave one of our students the opportunity to work in the registration area of the History and Technology Department. Another student assisted with the Bunjilaka Redevelopment Project, Collection Registration, re-housing of Exhibition Materials, and research and assessment of the Maori Collections.

National Gallery of Victoria The first student here worked on the fashion exhibition Black. She worked on the installation, documentation and carried out research for the catalogue. The second student, who had a background in law, worked on a report on the implementation of a policy on intellectual property. A third student worked on the annual secondary students’ art prize d that the NGV runs.

The Victorian Police Museum: Our student worked on the research for the museum’s first temporary and travelling exhibition Ambush: The Kelly Gang Murders at Stringybark Creek.

Art Gallery of NSW A student worked on Indian manuscripts for an upcoming exhibition

City of Sydney Archives A student worked on collection management

Powerhouse Museum: Two students recently working on research for this museum

In the past students have interned at: Migration Museum of South Australia, West Australian Museum South Australia Museum Macleay Museum, University of Sydney, Museum of Australia: Kenmore Hospital Museum, Goulburn, B’ula B’ula Arts Community, NT, National Parks and Wildlife (NSW)

Within ANU:

Research School of Humanities and the Arts : A visual anthropology student worked with small independent filmmakers Pip Deveson and Kim McKenzie editing nine hours of film to a more modest workable scale. Also a student worked on the private collection of anthropologist Dr Louise Hamby to document and photograph the indigenous art collection. The internship also included writing exhibition rationale of the fibre works. A student worked with filmmaker Dr David McDougal.

A student accompanied Dr Melinda Hinkson to film and photograph a project which become an exhibition and book.

A student worked on visitor evaluation and transcribing interviews with Dr Laurajane Smith, Future Fellow ANU

IPPHA (Institute for Professional Practice in Heritage and the Arts) Three students headed off to Kakadu National Park to work on the Cultural Heritage Management Plan. This project is under the auspices of IPPHA, based in the Research School of Humanities and the Arts at ANU. The students had the daunting task of developing a data audit for the project. This project continued back in Canberra.

In the past : Division of Pacific and Asian History: Dr Chris Ballard; and the Research School of Pacific studies: Dr Ian Maidmont; and the Classics Museum: Dr Elizabeth Minchin

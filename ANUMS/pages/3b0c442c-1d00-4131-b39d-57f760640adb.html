Centenary of Canberra

In 2013, we mark 100 years since the naming of Canberra, our national capital and home of the Australian story. The Centenary of Canberra offers an opportunity for Australians to revisit and re-imagine their national capital—the city that tells the story of our country’s freedom, spirit, achievements and aspirations. Visit www.canberra100.com.au for the year-long program of celebrations.

The ANU School of Art is providing a number of activites that links itself as both an education and cultural provider that has contributed to shaping the city and life of Canberra. The School of Art has a long history of generating inclusive and participatory outreach programs with Canberra’s arts and general communities.

 

Centenary Outreach  Activities

Speakers Lois Conner and Jessica Einhorn

Selling Yarns 3: Weaving the Nation’s Story

28 February – 3 March 2013

A four day conference with national and international speaker, this conference focuses on the transferral of knowledge in modern society, collaborative partnerships, fashion and fabric and contemporary modes of practice. Indigenous artists from remote and urban communities from across Australia will diverge on Canberra to participate in the event and will deliver a stimulating program showcasing contemporary Indigenous craft and design.

This event is a partnership between the Australian National University, Craft and ACT: Craft and Design Centres and the National Museum of Australia.

The tapestry in progress

Canberra Community Tapestry

The Canberra Community Centenary Tapestry is an 18 month community arts project which will create a large scale woven tapestry to celebrate Canberra’s 100th birthday in 2013. The project has been funded by the Canberra 100 Community Initiatives Fund, with support from the Legislative Assembly of the ACT and the Textiles Workshop at the Australian National University School of Art. Under the leadership of Head of Workshop and internationally acclaimed artist Valerie Kirk, and with input from the Canberra community this project acknowledges Canberra’s past, present and future. Free community workshop, talks and visits are taking place over an 18 month period as the tapestry emerges under the fingers of the participants in the project, with Tapestry Coordinator Dan Edwards. Visit the Canberra Centenary Community Tapestry website.

Artists in Residency

Two Artists in Residency projects that explore the role of the art school in Canberra’s cultural history.

The Art School Anecdote

Zoe Walker and Neil Bromwich – The Art School Anecdote

In March, ANU School of Art invited Walker and Bromwich to return to the School, to which they have been regular visitors for over ten years, and take part in the Centenary Residency Program. In collaboration with Canberra Contemporary Arts Space, and with the assistance of Arts ACT, they worked closely with students, staff and a diverse range of practising artist to creating a performance event that reflected upon the history of the School and the role it has played, and continues to play, in the life of the city.

The Art School Anecdote played out on Friday March 22 and included  live art, performance, anecdote and re-enactment by past and current art students, re-telling histories and refreshing memories in a playfully eccentric style. Participating artists and performers included Blaide Lallemand, John Reid, Ellis Hutch and Caroline Huf, Kirsten Farrell, David Williams, Martyn Jolly, David Keany, Dennis Finn, Frank Maconochie, Claire Granata, Gordon Bull amongst many others.

Barbara Campbell – Activating ACT 3 (1982 – 2013)

Canberra: Ten Australian Performance Artists (directed by Ingo Kleinert, then a lecturer of the Canberra School of Art) took place around the campus of the Canberra School of Art and, in works by Mike Parr and Stelarc, a little further afield. The event took place in October 1982 and coincided with the opening of the Australian National Gallery. As such, Canberra was full of interstate and international visitors in town for a big weekend of art. Barbara Campbell will work with School of Art students to investigate this important art event and develop a new response that highlights Canberra’s role in fostering contemporary art through venues such as the ANU School of Art.

SCULPTURE: Place and Space

International symposium SCULPTURE: Place and Space, will address the future of sculpture in the realm of public spaces beyond the convention of public art. Land Art is a practise whereby the material of the place makes the form of the work. The symposium will address the balance of iconic land art that became the genesis of re thinking the environment and where contemporary sculptural practise focuses on a ‘light touch’ upon the earth. This symposium will address practises of sculpture in our environment (public places), land as material and context and the evolution of Environment Art.

SCULPTURE: Place and Space will take place at the National Gallery of Australia over May 10, 11 and 12. To find out more and register for the symposium, visit the symposium event page.

Melanie Fitzmaurice - Explorer's Pack

Making Tracks - Sculpture Alumni Exhibition

To coincide with the major NGA and ANU School of Art symposium SCULPTURE: Place and Space, the School of Art Gallery will host an exhibition that demonstrates the diversity of practise and approaches to making sculpture by thirty years of sculpture alumni.

The exhibition features Michele Beevors, Joel Bliss, Rachel Bowak, Jacqueline Bradley, Melanie Fitzmaurice, Cristy Gilbert and Anna Simic, Jay Kochel, Owen Lewis , Pamela Lofts, Noelene Lucas, Anne Neil, Louis Pratt, Simon Scheuerle, Camille Serisier, Amanda Stuart and Peter Van der Mark.

Making Tracks will open on Friday 10th May.

but mostly air

An exhibition of work by alumni - August 9 to September 7

The ANU School of Art has a stellar alumni of artists engaged at the forefront of contemporary Australian art practise, all of which germinated here in the environs of the School of Art and in Canberra. Guest curator and alumni of the School of Art, Jason Smith (Director of The Heidi Museum of Modern Art) will curate a feature exhibition of ANU School of Art Alumni. Reaching out to local Canberrans and to the nation, this exhibition spans a period over twenty years and demonstrate the strength of art practise that has evolved from the School of Art. It will celebrate the continuing visionary teaching program of the School that commenced in 1976 as a fully fledged and independent Art School.

The theremin in action, 1975

Theremin Project

In 1971 and 1972 Australian media artist Stanislaus Ostoja-Kotkowski undertook an ANU Creative Arts Fellowship which resulted in the design and production of a set of theremins which also constituted a series of interactive, abstract 'paintings'. These were performed on by notable musicians and composers, including Larry Sitsky at the significant Australia 75 Festival of the Creative Arts and Sciences held in Canberra. The theremins fell into disrepair however one was recently recovered from a rubbish skip by a knowledgeable member of staff. The eminent Australian media artist and historian Dr Stephen Jones, will visit the School of Art three times in the second part of the year: twice to work on different stages of the theremin’s re-build in the electronics laboratory of the Research School of Engineering in collaboration with students, and once to present the finished theremin at a public event at the School of Art Gallery.

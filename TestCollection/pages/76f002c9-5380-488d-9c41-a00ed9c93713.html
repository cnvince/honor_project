ANU Sustainability » Landscape & biodiversity

Landscape & biodiversity

The natural landscape is a dominant feature of the 145 hectare Acton campus, which is home to a rich diversity of native plant and animal species. Located adjacent to Black Mountain Nature Reserve and the Australian National Botanic Gardens, the campus forms part of an important ecological corridor for local biodiversity. Sullivans Creek, which runs through the centre of campus, and Lake Burley Griffin, along the southern border of campus, are other important landscape features.

The Acton Campus is renowned for the age and significance of it's remnant trees. A large applebox on the western side of campus was estimated to be more than 300 years old by the late Prof. John Banks, one of Australia's foremost tree experts. In addition to enhancing the aesthetics of the campus, the trees and green landscapes offer shade and help to minimise the urban heat island effect, while also storing and sequestering carbon.

Supporting urban biodiversity

Extensive surveys have highlighted the significance of the Acton campus as a home to native biodiversity and the importance of maintaining vital habitat and ecosystems. The mix of aquatic and land ecosystems across campus supports a range of native wildlife including:

  • reptiles such as turtles and water dragons
  • the striped marsh frog and several other species of native frog
  • gang gang cockatoos, cormorants and an extensive variety of other birds
  • mammals such as the brushtail and ringtail possums and flying foxes
  • the water rat and platypus, Australia's only two native amphibious mammals, have both been sighted on campus.

Campus as classroom

Home to some of the most significant open landscape areas adjacent to the Canberra City centre, the campus serves as an important recreational and educational setting for the University and local community. The landscape is the ideal study environment for students who are learning about the interconnections between the natural environment and modern societies. Budding researchers gain practical experience in collecting data and assessing first-hand the impact of various environmental management initiatives.

Water in the landscape

To make the landscape more resilient to drought and contribute to regional responsible water-use initiatives, the University has set ambitious targets to eliminate the use of potable water from the landscape. A key priority of the ANUgreen program is to achieve a sustainable campus by maintaining the biodiversity and amenity values of the campus landscape, while reducing the amount of water required for irrigation.

Pollution & water quality

The final stretch of Sullivan's Creek, one of Canberra's major urban waterways and a nexus for much of the stormwater from the northern suburbs, flows through the Acton campus and connects with Lake Burley Griffin. The enhancement and expansion of wetlands along the Creek has increased the capacity of these natural features to act as bio-filtration systems to improve water quality. They have also provided important habitat for a range of native aquatic wildlife such as frogs, water dragons and various bird species.

Sustainable tips

Everyone can help conserve the landscape and protect the species that call ANU home.

  1. Avoid walking, driving or parking on planted areas
  2. Do not litter
  3. Drive slowly on campus and give way to wildlife crossing roads

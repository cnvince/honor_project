The NHMRC open access policy and you

What does the policy say?

The National Health and Medical Research Council (NHMRC) revised policy on the dissemination of research findings came into effect on
1 July 2012. It states:

NHMRC therefore requires that any publications arising from an NHMRC supported research project must be deposited into an open access institutional repository within a twelve month period from the date of publication.

>> more information

 

  • NHMRC revised policy on the dissemination of research findings
  • CAUL Open Scholarship Initiative Advisory Committee NHMRC FAQ

What is the policy asking for?

While the online statement currently says 'any publications', the NHMRC have clarified they are only requiring journal articles resulting from NHMRC supported grants be made available through an institutional repository.

  • Further information on the policy sent from NHMRC to DVCs of Research, Heads of research offices, Librarians and AAMRI Heads - Sept 2012

What is an institutional repository?

Institutional repositories are databases which hold and disseminate scholarly research material. All records have permanently citable URLs. The records and full text of the research outputs are fully indexed by search engines, making work not only open access but highly visible.

The institutional repository at the Australian National University is the ANU Research repository, which provides statistics about the level of use of each record. Work can be submitted through the simple web form on the ANU Open Access pages.

  • Submission form for ANU Research repository

When do I need to make my work available?

Articles published in July 2012 will need to be available in July 2013. But we do not recommend waiting until then to start the process of making your work available.

To which grants does this policy apply?

The policy relates to any publications after 1 July 2012 - regardless of the grant the publication is attached to.

Do publishers allow us to do this?

Yes, to varying degrees. The repository team will check the copyright arrangements for each item. People who are interested in investigating the copyright status of an article or publisher can use the service at the Sherpa/RoMEO site.

We may need to contact authors about obtaining a different version of the work to make open access.

  • Sherpa/RoMEO site for copyright checking

What is the difference between a Submitted, an Accepted or a Published Version?

The Published Version is the publisher's PDF. It is sometimes called the 'Version of Record'. In most cases we cannot make this version open access immediately after publication.

The Accepted Version is your final peer reviewed and corrected version. The text is the same at the final version, but without the formatting. This is the version we are most likely to be able to make available.

Allowing for an embargo, we should be able to make approximately 70% of NHMRC funded research available in the repository.

The Submitted Version is the version you send to the publisher for peer review.

  • NISO Journal Article Versions

If the research was undertaken by several institutions, do they all need to hold a copy of the work?

The policy states that it is the responsibility of the Chief Investigator A to make the journal articles from a grant available. This does not prevent authors at other institutions from also depositing the work in their institutional repository.

What if the work is openly accessible through some other means?

If the work is available through an open access journal, or deposited in a subject-based repository (such as arXiv.org, SSRN or PubMed Central) the metadata (citation information) about the work must still be entered in the institutional repository. We will link to the open access version. This is to allow the NHMRC to monitor what articles are being made open access.

What if I published my work with a publisher that does not allow open access?

The metadata (citation information) about the work must still be entered in the institutional repository. The repository team will check the copyright status of the work. If we are not able to make it available the record will hold information about the copyright restrictions on the work, and the item will be held 'closed'. The repository staff will provide the author with the wording about the copyright situation for the author to include in their Final Report submission.

Should authors consider open access policies of publishers when choosing publication options?

The policy is not designed to change the journals authors choose to publish in. Note however that the ANU does not encourage researchers to publish with journals which do not allow provision of open access to research through a repository. The largest publisher which does not allow the deposit of items in repositories is Wiley Blackwell.

What about the Australian Research Council?

The Australian Research Council (ARC) has introduced an open access policy for all publications arising from the grants allocated in 2013 onwards. As this policy will relate specifically to the grants from 2013, there will be an implementation period of several years. This policy proposal has been discussed with universities.

  • ARC Open Access Policy

Are people who hold scholarships from NHMRC covered by this open access policy?

In this case no. Students are covered in principle as grant recipients but the NHMRC have indicated that deposit by them will not be mandatory at this stage because of the (often long) lead times involved.

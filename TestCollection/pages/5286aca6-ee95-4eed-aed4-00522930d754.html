Researcher's projects

Change in language, culture and identity: lessons from small isolated communities

Palmerston Island is a tiny atoll in the Cook Islands group, 2km in diameter and inhabited by 54 people.  It is extremely isolated, with no regular scheduled transport, and only one satellite connection providing communication with the rest of the Cook Islands.  The island is visited by private yachts once or twice a week during August-September, and by irregular supply boats two to three times a year.  It was settled in the 1850s by an Englishman, William Marsters, three Cook Island women, and a Portuguese sailor.  The current Palmerston Islanders are descended from this tiny group, and are monolingual in a dialect of English that has distinctive phonetic and grammatical influences from a Northern English dialect, Cook Island Maori, and Polynesian English. 

There is a great deal of variation in Palmerston Island English, both between speakers, between families, and within each individual speaker's use of language. By investigating the current linguistic and social situation on the island, and tracing its origins, I am trying to understand how new dialects develop, mechanisms of change in language contact situations, and the relationship between social and linguistic networks.  

The project is supported by a Discovery Grant from the Australian Research Council and an APD postdoctoral fellowship.

Documentation of Tokodede, a language of East Timor

This project is joint work in progress together with Antoinette Schapper, University of Leiden. 

Tokodede is an Austronesian languages spoken in the western border region of Timor-Leste.There has been no previous systematic work or description of this language. Like other minority languages of the fledgling nation of Timor-Leste, Tokodede is rapidly losing ground to the national languages, especially Tetun.

From a language contact perspective, Tokodede is extremely interesting because various dialects have differing degrees of contact with other languages of the region. Tokodede spoken in Liquiçá, for example, is in close contact with, and often mixed with, Mambai, while this is not the case for the varieties spoken around Maubara.

This project seeks to foster the Tokodede language by carrying out documentation and producing educational resources for use in line with the new Program on Mother Tongue Education. An initial field trip was completed in June/July 2012 and funding is currently being sought for future work.

Tracing change in family and social organization in Indigenous Australia, using evidence from language

Indigenous society has been founded on kinship systems quite different from those of the Europeans and on social categories unique to Australia, like sections and subsections, binding distant people together in family-like relationships. The project reconstructed Australian Indigenous social organization over the last several millennia using comparative linguistics and anthropology.  It builds on an ethnographic sample across Australia, together with a large database of family and social vocabulary from Indigenous languages, which has been automated to provide rapid data entry, analysis and mapping. The results of this project help us to understand how these social systems have changed, and the impact on Indigenous people’s lives.

This project was supported by an ARC Discovery Grant, with CIs Harold Koch, Ian Keen and PI Laurent Dousset. Other project members were Patrick McConvell, Claire Bowern and Barry Alpher. I ended my official employment on this project in early 2011, but some publications are still being finalised.

Legal Liability under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Cth)

Purpose

To inform staff of their legal liability under the new Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Cth).

Procedure
1. Under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (the Act) the Australian National University (ANU) as the person conducting the business or undertaking, its officers, workers and other persons all have non-transferable, legally enforceable, duties (see: Appendix 1 for definitions). ANU, its officers, workers and other persons may be subject to criminal prosecution where duties prescribed by the Act are not met.
DUTIES

ANU

2. The duties of ANU under the Act are to:
Â· provide and maintain a work environment without risks to health or safety
Â· provide and maintain safe plant and structures
Â· provide and maintain safe systems of work
Â· ensure the safe use, handling, storage and transport of plant, structures substances
Â· provide adequate facilities for the welfare at work of workers, including ensuring access to those facilities
Â· provide any information, training, instruction or supervision necessary to protect all persons from risks to their health and safety
Â· monitor the health of workers and the conditions at the workplace to prevent illness or injury of workers.
Officers
3. The Act places a positive duty on officers to exercise due diligence to ensure that ANU complies with any duty or obligation under the Act. In exercising due diligence officers must take reasonable steps to:
Â· acquire and keep up to date knowledge of work health and safety matters
Â· gain an understanding of the nature business and generally of the associated hazards and risks
Â· ensure ANU has appropriate resources and processes to identify hazards and eliminate or minimise risks associated with those hazards
Â· ensure that ANU has appropriate processes for receiving and considering information regarding incidents, hazards and risks and responding in a timely way to that information
Â· ensure that ANU has, and implements, processes for complying with any duty or obligation it has under the Act and
Â· verify the provision and use of the resources and processes for all of these.
Note. This list is not an exhaustive list as there may be other things required for an officer to show that they have exercised due diligence in the particular circumstances.
Workers
4. Workers have four duties under the Act. They must:
Â· take reasonable care for their own health and safety and
Â· take reasonable care that their acts or omissions do not affect the health and safety of others and
Â· comply with any reasonable instruction to comply with the Act given by ANU and
Â· comply with any reasonable policy or procedure relating to work health and safety.
Other persons
5. Under the Act other persons on ANU premises have three duties. They must:
Â· take reasonable care for their own health and safety and
Â· take reasonable care that their acts or omissions do not affect the health and safety of others and
Â· comply with any reasonable instruction to comply with the Act given by ANU.
OFFENCES AND PENALTIES
6. Under the Act there are three categories of breaches.
Category 1 (reckless conduct) - a person has a health and safety duty, and
Â· without reasonable excuse, engages in conduct that exposes an individual to whom that duty is owed to a risk of death or serious injury or illness and
Â· is reckless as to the risk to an individual of death or serious injury or illness.
Category 2 (failure to comply with health and safety duty) - a person has a health and safety duty, and
Â· the person fails to comply with that duty, and
Â· the failure exposes an individual to a risk of death or serious injury or illness.
Category 3 (failure to comply with health and safety duty) - a person has a health and safety duty, and they fail to comply with that duty.
7. The Table below provides further information on the penalties for the breaches.

Category

Description

Maximum Penalty

Category 1

Breach of a health and safety duty and the person is reckless as to risk of death or serious injury or illness to that individual.

Exceptions
8. A volunteer does not commit an offence for a failure to comply with a health and safety duty, except a duty which applies to workers or other persons.
OTHER DUTIES AND ASSOCIATED PENALTIES
9. Other duties under the Act relate to:
Â· notifiable incidents
Â· authorisation of workplaces
Â· consultation, cooperation and coordination between duty holders
Â· work groups and Health and Safety Representatives (HSRs)
Â· health and safety committees
Â· provisional improvement notices
Â· discriminatory, coercive and misleading conduct
Â· misrepresentation
Â· entry permit holders
Â· powers of the regulator to obtain information
Â· improvement and prohibition notices
Â· undertakings
Â· confidentiality of information and
Â· levying workers in relation to work health and safety.
See: Appendix 1 for further details relating to penalties associated with these duties.
10. Unless otherwise stated in the Appendix, all penalties are criminal penalties. Failure to comply with the duties in Appendix 1 may also lead to criminal prosecution.
Appendix 1
DEFINITIONS
11. The following definitions apply in this procedure.
Officer
12. An âOfficer' is a person who makes, or participates in making, decisions that affect the whole, or a substantial part, of the business or undertaking of ANU . This definition is the same as the Corporations Act. At ANU officers include:
Â· The University Council,
Â· The University Executive, and
Â· Heads of Colleges.
13. It may also include others including, for example Service Division Directors, Directors of Research Schools and any member of ANU who has responsibility for making decisions that affect the whole, or a substantial part, of ANU operations.

Worker
14. The Act adopts a broad definition of âworker' that extends beyond the traditional employment relationship to include any person who works, in any capacity, in or as part of the University. An example of where this has implications for the University is when work groups select HSRs. A practical result is that a contractor, being a worker, could represent a work group.
15. Workers include:
Â· an employee or
Â· some post-graduate students or
Â· a contractor or sub-contractor or
Â· an employee of a contractor or sub-contractor or
Â· an employee of a labour hire company who has been assigned to work in the person's business or undertaking or
Â· an outworker (someone working from home) or
Â· an apprentice or trainee or
Â· a student gaining work experience or
Â· a volunteer.
Other persons
16. Other persons comprise:
Â· undergraduate students and
Â· a postgraduate student and
Â· visitors to ANU.
OTHER DUTIES AND ASSOCIATED PENALTIES
17. The following is only a guide to the Act and is not meant to be comprehensive.
18. All penalties are criminal penalties, except where otherwise indicated.
Notifiable incidents
19. ANU must ensure that Comcare is notified immediately after becoming aware that a notifiable incident arising out of the conduct of the business or undertaking has occurred.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
20. ANU must keep a record of each notifiable incident for at least 5 years from the day that notice of the incident is given to Comcare under this section.
Penalty: individual-$5,000, body corporate-$25,000.
21. The person with management or control of a workplace at which a notifiable incident has occurred must ensure so far as is reasonably practicable, that the site where the incident occurred is not disturbed until an inspector arrives at the site or any earlier time that an inspector directs. The site includes any plant, substance, structure or thing associated with the notifiable incident.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
Authorisation of workplaces
22. ANU must not conduct a business or undertaking at a workplace or direct or allow a worker to carry out work at a workplace if the regulations require the workplace or workplaces in that class of workplace to be authorised and the workplace is not authorised in accordance with the regulations.
Note. Authorised means authorised by a licence, permit, registration or other authority (however described) as required by the regulations.
Penalty: individual-$50,000, body corporate-$250,000.
23. A person must not use plant or a substance at a workplace if the regulations require the plant or substance or its design to be authorised and the plant or substance or its design is not authorised in accordance with the regulations.
Penalty: individual-$20,000, body corporate-$100,000.
24. ANU must not direct or allow a worker to use the plant or substance at a workplace if the regulations require the plant or substance or its design to be authorised and the plant or substance or its design is not authorised in accordance with the regulations.
Penalty: individual-$20,000, body corporate-$100,000.
25. A person must not carry out work at a workplace if:
Â· the regulations require the work, or class of work, to be carried out by, or on behalf of, a person who is authorised; and
Â· the person, or the person on whose behalf the work is carried out, is not authorised in accordance with the regulations.
Penalty: individual-$20,000, body corporate-$100,000.
26. ANU must not direct or allow a worker to carry out work at a workplace if the regulations require the work, or class of work, to be carried out by, or on behalf of, a person who is authorised and the person, or the person on whose behalf the work is to be carried out, is not authorised in accordance with the regulations.
Penalty: individual-$20,000, body corporate-$100,000.
27. A person must not carry out work at a workplace if the regulations require the work, or class of work, to be carried out by, or under the supervision of, a person who has prescribed qualifications or experience, and the person does not have the prescribed qualifications or experience, or the work is not carried out under the supervision of a person who has the prescribed qualifications or experience.
Penalty: individual-$20,000, body corporate-$100,000.
28. ANU must not direct or allow a worker to carry out work at a workplace if the regulations require the work, or class of work, to be carried out by, or under the supervision of, a person who has prescribed qualifications or experience, and the worker does not have the prescribed qualifications or experience, or the work is not carried out under the supervision of a person who has the prescribed qualifications or experience.
Penalty: individual-$20,000, body corporate-$100,000.
29. A person must comply with the conditions of any authorisation given to that person under the regulations.
Penalty: individual-$20,000, body corporate-$100,000.
Consultation, cooperation and coordination between duty holders
30. Consultation with other duty holders is required in relation to the following health and safety matters:
Â· when identifying hazards and assessing risks to health and safety arising from the work carried out or to be carried out by the business or undertaking;
Â· when making decisions about ways to eliminate or minimise those risks;
Â· when making decisions about the adequacy of facilities for the welfare of workers;
Â· when proposing changes that may affect the health or safety of workers;
Â· when making decisions about the procedures for consulting with workers; or resolving work health or safety issues at the workplace; or monitoring the health of workers; or monitoring the conditions at any workplace under the management or control of the person conducting the business or undertaking; or providing information and training for workers;
Â· when carrying out any other activity relating to consultation prescribed by the regulations.
31. If more than one person has a duty in relation to the same matter under the Act, each person with the duty must, so far as is reasonably practicable, consult, cooperate and coordinate activities with all other persons who have a duty in relation to the same matter.
Penalty: individual-$20,000, body corporate-$100,000.
32. ANU must, so far as is reasonably practicable, consult with workers who carry out its work who are, or are likely to be, directly affected by a matter relating to work health or safety.
Penalty: individual-$20,000, body corporate-$100,000.
Work groups and Health and Safety Representatives (HSRs)
33. A work group is to be determined by negotiation and agreement between ANU and the workers who will form the work group, or their representatives.
34. ANU must, if asked by a worker, negotiate with the worker's representative in negotiations under this section (including negotiations for a variation of an agreement) and must not exclude the representative from those negotiations.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
35. ANU must notify the workers of the outcome of the negotiations and of any work groups determined by agreement as soon as practicable after the negotiations are completed. ANU must also notify workers of the outcomes of negotiations for the variation of an agreement concerning the determination of a work group or groups as soon as practicable after the negotiations are completed.
Penalty: individual-$2,000, body corporate-$10,000.
36. 22 and 23 apply also in the case where there is negotiation for work groups for multiple businesses, with the same penalties for non-compliance. Note that negotiations here must consider only:
Â· the number and composition of work groups to be represented by HSRs
Â· the number of HSRs and deputy HSRs (if any) for each work group
Â· the workplace or workplaces to which the work groups will apply and
Â· the businesses or undertakings to which the work groups will apply.
37. The workers in a work group may determine how an election of a HSR for the work group is to be conducted. Any election must comply with the procedures (if any) prescribed by the regulations.
38. If a majority of the workers in a work group so determine, the election may be conducted with the assistance of a union or other person or organisation.
39. ANU must provide any resources, facilities and assistance that are reasonably necessary or are prescribed by the regulations to enable elections to be conducted.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
40. ANU must:
Â· consult, so far as is reasonably practicable, on work health and safety matters with any HSR for a work group of workers carrying out work for the business or undertaking and
Â· confer with a HSR for a work group, whenever reasonably requested by the representative, for the purpose of ensuring the health and safety of the workers in the work group and
Â· allow any HSR for the work group to have access to information that the person has relating to:
o hazards (including associated risks) at the workplace affecting workers in the work group; and
o the health and safety of the workers in the work group; and
Â· with the consent of a worker that the HSR represents, allow the HSR to be present at an interview concerning work health and safety between the worker and:
o an inspector; or
o the person conducting the business or undertaking at that workplace or the person's representative; and
Â· with the consent of one or more workers that the HSR represents, allow the HSR to be present at an interview concerning work health and safety between a group of workers, which includes the workers who gave the consent, and:
o an inspector; or
o the person conducting the business or undertaking at that workplace or the person's representative; and
Â· provide any resources, facilities and assistance to a HSR for the work group that are reasonably necessary or prescribed by the regulations to enable the representative to exercise their powers or perform their functions under the Act; and
Â· allow a person assisting a HSR for the work group to have access to the workplace if that is necessary to enable the assistance to be provided; and
Â· permit a HSR for the work group to accompany an inspector during an inspection of any part of the workplace where a worker in the work group works; and
Â· provide any other assistance to the HSR for the work group that may be required by the regulations.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
41. ANU must allow a HSR to spend such time as is reasonably necessary to exercise their powers and perform their functions under the Act.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
42. ANU must not allow a HSR to have access to any personal or medical information concerning a worker without the worker's consent unless the information is in a form that:
Â· does not identify the worker and
Â· could not reasonably be expected to lead to the identification of the worker.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
43. ANU must:
Â· as soon as practicable within the period of 3 months after the request is made, allow the HSR time off work to attend the course of training; and
Â· pay the course fees and any other reasonable costs associated with the HSR's attendance at the course of training.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
44. ANU must ensure that:
Â· a list of each HSR and deputy HSR (if any) for each work group of workers carrying out work for the business or undertaking is prepared and kept up to date and
Â· a copy of the update list is displayed:
Â· at the principal place of business of the business or undertaking and
Â· at any other workplace that is appropriate taking into account the constitution of the relevant work group or work groups in a manner that is readily accessible to workers in the relevant work group or work groups.
Penalty: individual-$2,000, body corporate-$10,000.
Health and safety committees
45. ANU must establish a health and safety committee for part of the business or undertaking:
Â· within 2 months after being requested to do so by a HSR for a work group of workers carrying out work at that workplace, or five or more workers at that workplace; or
Â· if required by the regulations to do so, within the time prescribed by the regulations.
Penalty: individual-$5,000, body corporate-$25,000.
Duties of ANU regarding health and safety committees
46. ANU must allow each member of the health and safety committee to spend the time that is reasonably necessary to attend meetings of the committee or to carry out functions as a member of the committee.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
47. ANU must allow the health and safety committee for a workplace to have access to information that the person has relating to:
Â· hazards (including associated risks) at the workplace and
Â· the health and safety of the workers at the workplace.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
48. Notwithstanding the requirement of 2.29 ANU must not allow the health and safety committee to have access to any personal or medical information concerning a worker without the worker's consent unless the information is in a form that:
Â· does not identify the worker and
Â· could not reasonably be expected to lead to the identification of the worker.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
Provisional improvement notices
49. A person to whom a provisional improvement notice is issued must as soon as practicable display a copy of the notice in a prominent place at or near the workplace, or part of the workplace, at which work is being carried out that is affected by the notice.
Penalty: individual-$5,000, body corporate-$25,000.
50. A person must not intentionally remove, destroy, damage or deface a provisional improvement notice displayed under 2.31 during the period that the notice is in force.
Penalty: individual-$5,000, body corporate-$25,000.
51. A person to whom a provisional improvement notice is issued must comply with the provisional improvement notice within the time specified in the notice, unless an inspector has been required to attend the workplace under section 101 of the Act.
Penalty: individual-$50,000, body corporate-$250 000.
Discriminatory conduct
52. A person must not engage in discriminatory conduct for a prohibited reason (see: sections 105 and 106 of the Act for definitions of discriminatory conduct and prohibited reason).
Penalty: individual-$100,000, body corporate-$500,000.
Note 1: A person commits an offence only if the reason referred to in section 106 was the dominant reason for the discriminatory conduct.
Note 2: Civil proceedings may be brought in relation to discriminatory conduct engaged in for a prohibited reason.
Note 3: For the purposes of the application of the Criminal Code in relation to an offence under this section, intention is the fault element for the physical element of engaging in discriminatory conduct.
53. A person must not request, instruct, induce, encourage, authorise or assist another person to engage in discriminatory conduct for a prohibited reason.
Penalty: individual-$100,000, body corporate-$500,000.
Note 1: Civil proceedings may be brought under this Part if a person requested, instructed, induced, encouraged, authorised or assisted another person to engage in discriminatory conduct for a prohibited reason.
Note 2: For the purposes of the application of the Criminal Code in relation to an offence under this section, intention is the fault element for the physical element of requesting, instructing, inducing, encouraging, authorising or assisting another person to engage in conduct.
54. A person must not organise or take, or threaten to organise or take, any action against another person with intent to coerce or induce the other person, or a third person:
Â· to exercise or not to exercise a power, or to propose to exercise or not to exercise a power, under the Act or
Â· to perform or not to perform a function, or to propose to perform or not to perform a function, under the Act or
Â· to exercise or not to exercise a power or perform a function, or to propose to exercise or not to exercise a power or perform a function, in a particular way or
Â· to refrain from seeking, or continuing to undertake, a role under the Act.
Penalty: individual-$100,000, body corporate-$500,000.
Note 1: Civil proceedings may be brought under this Part in relation to a contravention of this section.
Note 2: In this section, a reference to taking action or threatening to take action against a person includes a reference to not taking a particular action or threatening not to take a particular action in relation to that person.
Note 3: To avoid doubt, a reasonable direction given by an emergency services worker in an emergency is not an action with intent to coerce or induce a person.
Misrepresentation
55. A person must not knowingly or recklessly make a false or misleading representation to another person about that other person's:
Â· rights or obligations under the Act or
Â· ability to initiate, or participate in, a process or proceedings under the Act or
Â· ability to make a complaint or inquiry to a person or body empowered under the Act to seek compliance with the Act.
Penalty: individual-$100,000, body corporate-$500,000.
Note. This section, (section 109) does not apply if the person to whom the representation is made would not be expected to rely on it.
WHS Entry Permit holders
56. A WHS entry permit holder may enter a workplace for the purpose of inquiring into a suspected contravention of the Act that relates to, or affects, a relevant worker.
57. The WHS entry permit holder must reasonably suspect before entering the workplace that the contravention has occurred or is occurring.
58. While at the workplace for this reason, the WHS entry permit holder may do all or any of the following in relation to the suspected contravention of the Act:
Â· inspect any work system, plant, substance, structure or other thing relevant to the suspected contravention
Â· consult with the relevant workers in relation to the suspected contravention
Â· consult with the relevant person conducting a business or undertaking about the suspected contravention
Â· require the relevant person conducting a business or undertaking to allow the WHS entry permit holder to inspect, and make copies of, any document that is directly relevant to the suspected contravention and that is kept at the workplace, or is accessible from a computer that is kept at the workplace
Â· warn any person whom the WHS entry permit holder reasonably believes to be exposed to a serious risk to his or her health or safety emanating from an immediate or imminent exposure to a hazard, of that risk.
59. ANU may refuse to allow the WHS entry permit holder to inspect or make copies of a document if to do so would contravene a law of the Commonwealth or a law of a State.
60. ANU must not, without reasonable excuse, refuse or fail to comply with a requirement to inspect or make copies of any document.
WHS civil penalty provision.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
Note 1: The Act places an evidential burden on the defendant to show a reasonable excuse.
Note 2: At least 24 hours' notice is required for an entry to a workplace to inspect employee records or other documents held by someone other than a person conducting a business or undertaking.
Note 3: The use or disclosure of personal information obtained under this section is regulated under the Privacy Act 1988.
61. Fair Work Australia may deal with a dispute about the exercise or purported exercise by a WHS entry permit holder of a right of entry under the Act (including a dispute about whether a request under section128 is reasonable).
62. Fair Work Australia may deal with the dispute in any manner it thinks fit, including by means of mediation, conciliation or arbitration.
63. If Fair Work Australia deals with the dispute by arbitration, it may make one or more of the following orders:
Â· an order imposing conditions on a WHS entry permit
Â· an order suspending a WHS entry permit
Â· an order revoking a WHS entry permit
Â· an order about the future issue of WHS entry permits to 1 or more persons
Â· any other order it considers appropriate.
64. A person must not contravene an order under this section (subsection 142(3))
WHS civil penalty provision.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
65. A person must not, without reasonable excuse, refuse or unduly delay entry into a workplace by a WHS entry permit holder who is entitled to enter the workplace.
WHS civil penalty provision.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
Note. The Act places an evidential burden on the accused to show a reasonable excuse.
66. A person must not intentionally and unreasonably hinder or obstruct a WHS entry permit holder in entering a workplace or in exercising any rights at a workplace.
WHS civil penalty provision.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
Powers of Comcare to obtain information
67. This section, (section 155), applies if Comcare has reasonable grounds to believe that a person is capable of giving information, providing documents or giving evidence in relation to a possible contravention of the Act or that will assist Comcare to monitor or enforce compliance with the Act.
68. Comcare may, by written notice served on the person, require the person to do one or more of the following:
Â· to give Comcare, in writing signed by the person (or in the case of a body corporate, by a competent officer of the body corporate) and within the time and in the manner specified in the notice, that information of which the person has knowledge
Â· to produce to Comcare, in accordance with the notice, those document;
Â· to appear before a person appointed by Comcare on a day, and at a time and place, specified in the notice (being a day, time and place that are reasonable in the circumstances) and give either orally or in writing that evidence and produce those documents.
Note. Comcare must not require a person to appear unless they have taken all reasonable steps to obtain the information and have been unable to do so.
69. A person must not, without reasonable excuse, refuse or fail to comply with a requirement under this section.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
Note. There is an evidential burden on the accused to show a reasonable excuse.
General powers of an inspector
70. An inspector who lawfully enters a workplace may do all or any of the following:
Â· inspect, examine and make inquiries at the workplace
Â· inspect and examine anything (including a document) at the workplace
Â· bring to the workplace and use any equipment or materials that may be required
Â· take measurements, conduct tests and make sketches or recordings (including photographs, films, audio, video, digital or other recordings)
Â· take and remove for analysis a sample of any substance or thing
Â· require a person at the workplace to give the inspector reasonable help to exercise the inspector's powers under the paragraphs above
Â· exercise any compliance power or other power that is reasonably necessary to be exercised by the inspector for the purposes of this Act.
71. A person required to give reasonable help to an inspector must not, without reasonable excuse, refuse or fail to comply with the requirement.
Note. The Act places an evidential burden on the accused to show a reasonable excuse.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
72. An inspector who enters a workplace under this Division may:
Â· require a person to tell the inspector who has custody of, or access to, a document or
Â· require a person who has custody of, or access to, a document to produce that document to the inspector while the inspector is at that workplace or within a specified period or
Â· require a person at the workplace to answer any questions put by the inspector.
73. A requirement to produce a document must be made by written notice unless the circumstances require the inspector to have immediate access to the document.
74. An interview conducted by an inspector under this section must be conducted in private if the inspector considers it appropriate; or the person being interviewed so requests. This may be invoked during an interview by the inspector; or the person being interviewed, in which case the remainder of the interview will be conducted in private.
Note. This does not prevent a representative of the person being interviewed from being present at the interview.
75. A person must not, without reasonable excuse, refuse or fail to comply with a requirement under this section, (section 171).
Note. The Act places an evidential burden on the accused to show a reasonable excuse.
Penalty: individual-$10, 000 body corporate-$50,000.
Note. See sections 172 and 173 of the Act in relation to self-incrimination and section 269 in relation to legal professional privilege.
76. To enable a thing to be seized, an inspector may require the person in control of it to take it to a stated reasonable place by a stated reasonable time; and if necessary, to remain in control of it at the stated place for a reasonable time.
77. The requirement must be made by written notice; or if for any reason it is not practicable to give the notice, may be made orally and confirmed by written notice as soon as practicable.
78. A further requirement may be made under this section in relation to the same thing if it is necessary and reasonable to make the further requirement.
79. The person must not, without reasonable excuse, refuse or fail to comply with a requirement under this section, (section 177).
Note. The Act places an evidential burden on the accused to show a reasonable excuse under section 177.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
80. Having seized a thing, an inspector may:
Â· move the thing from the place where it was seized (the place of seizure) or
Â· leave the thing at the place of seizure but take reasonable action to restrict access to it; or
Example 1: Sealing a thing and marking it to show access to it is restricted.
Example 2: Sealing the entrance to a room where the seized thing is situated and marking it to show access to it is restricted.
Â· if the thing is plant or a structure-dismantle or cause to be dismantled the plant or structure.
81. If an inspector restricts access to a seized thing, a person must not tamper, or attempt to tamper, with the thing or something restricting access to the thing without an inspector's approval.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
82. An inspector may require a person to provide their name and residential address if:
Â· the inspector finds the person committing an offence against the Act or
Â· the inspector finds the person in circumstances that lead, or has information that leads, the inspector to reasonably suspect the person has committed an offence against this Act.
83. When asking a person to provide his or her name and residential address, the inspector must:
Â· tell the person the reason for the requirement to provide his or her name and residential address and
Â· warn the person that it is an offence to fail to state that name and residential address, unless the person has a reasonable excuse.
84. If the inspector reasonably believes that the name or residential address is false, the inspector may require the person to give evidence of its correctness.
85. A person must not, without reasonable excuse, refuse or fail to comply with a requirement under this section, (subsection 185(1) or 185(3)).
Note. The Act places an evidential burden on the accused to show a reasonable excuse under subsection 185(4).
Penalty: $10,000.
86. A person must not intentionally hinder or obstruct an inspector in exercising his or her compliance powers, or induce or attempt to induce any other person to do so.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
87. A person who is not an inspector must not, in any way, recklessly hold himself or herself out to be an inspector.
Penalty: $10,000.
88. A person commits an offence if:
Â· the person engages in conduct and
Â· the person intends, by engaging in that conduct, to directly or indirectly assault, threaten or intimidate another person and
Â· the other person is an inspector or a person assisting an inspector.
Penalty: individual-$50,000 or imprisonment for 2 years or both, body corporate-$250,000.
Compliance with notices and undertakings
89. The person to whom an improvement notice is issued, by an inspector, must comply with the notice within the period specified in the notice.
Penalty: individual-$50,000, body corporate-$250,000.
90. This section applies if an inspector reasonably believes that:
Â· an activity is occurring at a workplace that involves or will involve a serious risk to the health or safety of a person emanating from an immediate or imminent exposure to a hazard or
Â· an activity may occur at a workplace that, if it occurs, will involve a serious risk to the health or safety of a person emanating from an immediate or imminent exposure to a hazard.
91. The inspector may give a person who has control over the activity a direction prohibiting the carrying on of the activity, or the carrying on of the activity in a specified way, until an inspector is satisfied that the matters that give or will give rise to the risk have been remedied.
92. The direction may be given orally, but must be confirmed by written notice (a prohibition notice) issued to the person as soon as practicable.
93. The person to whom a direction is given or a prohibition notice is issued by an inspector, must comply with the direction or notice.
Penalty: individual-$100,000, body corporate-$500,000.
94. An inspector may issue a non-disturbance notice to the person with management or control of a workplace if the inspector reasonably believes that it is necessary to do so to facilitate the exercise of their compliance powers.
95. A person must not, without reasonable excuse, refuse or fail to comply with a non-disturbance notice issued to the person.
Note. The Act places an evidential burden on the accused to show a reasonable excuse.
Penalty: individual-$50,000, body corporate-$250,000.
96. A person to whom a notice is issued must, as soon as possible, display a copy of the notice in a prominent place at or near the workplace, or part of the workplace, at which work is being carried out that is affected by the notice.
Penalty: individual-$5,000, body corporate-$25,000.
97. A person must not intentionally remove, destroy, damage or deface a non-disturbance notice while the notice is in force.
Penalty: individual-$5,000, body corporate-$25,000.
WHS undertakings
98. A person must not contravene a WHS undertaking made by that person that is in effect.
Penalty: individual-$50,000, body corporate-$250,000.
Confidentiality of information
99. This section, (section 271) applies if a person obtains information or gains access to a document in exercising any power or function under the Act (other than under Part7).
100. Under subsection 271(2) the person must not do any of the following:
Â· disclose to anyone else the information or the contents of or information contained in the document
Â· give access to the document to anyone else
Â· use the information or document for any purpose.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
101. Subsection 271(2) does not apply to the disclosure of information, or the giving of access to a document or the use of information or a document:
Â· about a person, with the person's consent or
Â· that is necessary for the exercise of a power or function under this Act or
Â· that is made or given by the regulator or a person authorised by the regulator if the regulator reasonably believes the disclosure, access or use:
o is necessary for administering, or monitoring or enforcing compliance with, this Act or

o is required for the exercise of a power or function under a corresponding WHS law or
Â· that is required by any court, tribunal, authority or person having lawful authority to require the production of documents or the answering of questions or
Â· that is required or authorised under a law or
Â· to a Minister.
102. A person commits an offence if:
Â· the person (the defendant) intentionally discloses to another person the name of an individual and
Â· the individual has made a complaint in relation to the person to whom the individual's name is disclosed and
Â· the defendant knows or is reckless as to that fact.
Note. The defendant does not commit an offence if the disclosure is made with the consent of the individual or is required under a law.
Penalty: individual-$10,000, body corporate-$50,000.
ANU not to levy workers
